What is Matrix Pokémon?

Matrix Pokémon is a fan-made tactical card game that fuses two legendary universes: the Pokémon Trading Card Game and Chess. Instead of drawing cards into a hand, you lay your entire deck face-down on the table in a grid — the Matrix. The battlefield IS your deck.

Every card on the Matrix is a mystery until revealed. Your Pokémon navigate through revealed paths, collect Energy, evolve, and battle — all on one giant living board.

Standard Rules Apply: Unless this guide specifies otherwise, all standard Pokémon TCG rules (Energy requirements, Weakness/Resistance, Abilities, Special Conditions) remain active during combat.

The Key Twist: Position is Power

This is what makes Matrix Pokémon unique. Your Pokémon's row on the Matrix determines how much damage the defending Pokémon takes. The closer the defender is to the front (Row 1), the more damage it receives. The further back it hides (Row 6), the better protected it is.

Starting the Game

Step 4 – Startup Reveal

Each player reveals 7 cards of their choice from their Matrix — you pick which ones to flip.

⚠️ Important: At least 1 Basic Pokémon must be among your 7 revealed cards!
If you don't reveal any Basic Pokémon, flip all your cards back over, reshuffle the Matrix, and reveal again.
💡 Smart Reveal: Reveal cards that are next to each other so your Pokémon can immediately reach Energy cards without having to jump over face-down walls.

// STEP 4 // Angle 2 — revealed cards open the first movement paths.

Step 5 – Set Your Active Pokémon

Place a coin on one of your revealed Basic Pokémon cards. That coin marks your Active Pokémon — the one that will attack and be attacked this game.

// STEP 5 // A coin marks the Active Pokémon on the Matrix. Simple and clear!

Step 6 – Who Goes First?

Flip a coin. Heads = Player 1 and goes first.

ℹ️ Balance Rule: Player 1 reveals 0 cards on their very first turn (no advantage for going first). Player 2 reveals 1 card on their first turn. From turn 2 onward both players reveal 1 card per turn.

💡 Think Before You Reveal! Every card you reveal also opens paths for your opponent. Reveal cards that help your Pokémon move but don't create shortcuts into your territory.

Phase 2: Move Pokémon

Your Pokémon move like the Queen in Chess:

  • Straight (up / down / left / right) or diagonally
  • Maximum 4 spaces per move in one direction — no mid-move direction changes
  • Can only move over revealed cards — face-down cards are impassable walls!
  • Each Pokémon may move at most once per turn

Movement order:

  1. Move your Active Pokémon first (optional)
  2. Then move all other revealed Pokémon (each once, optional)
  3. Pokémon may also stay put — movement is never mandatory

06
Energy & Evolution

⚡ Collecting Energy

Energy Cards on the Matrix work like items on a game board. When a Pokémon lands on an Energy Card:

  • The Energy Card is placed under the Pokémon (attached, just like in regular TCG)
  • Each Pokémon can collect at most 1 Energy Card per turn
  • This applies to all your Pokémon on the Matrix — not just the Active one!
💡 Charge in the Back: Move your non-active Pokémon toward Energy Cards while your Active Pokémon fights. This way, your next attacker charges up safely in the back rows before advancing.

🔼 Evolution

When your Pokémon lands on a matching Evolution Card:

  1. Place the Evolution Card on top of the Basic Pokémon card
  2. The evolved Pokémon immediately has its new attacks and maximum HP
  3. A Pokémon can evolve once per turn
ℹ️ Evolution Cards on the Matrix: Stage 1 and Stage 2 Evolution cards sit on the Matrix like waiting items. They cannot move on their own — a Pokémon must walk to them to evolve.

Battle & Damage

⚔️ The Core Rule: Position is Armor

The damage a defending Pokémon takes depends on its own row position. The further back the defender stands, the more protection it has — like armor that gets stronger the deeper it is behind the front line.

// OFFICIAL DAMAGE FORMULA //
Effective Damage = Base Damage − (Defender's Row − 1) × 10

➜ Only the defender's row determines protection. Min. damage = 0.

Tracking Damage with Dice

Place dice on top of the damaged Pokémon card:

  • 🎲 1 pip = 10 HP damage
  • Example: 50 effective damage → place 5 dice on the Pokémon
  • When the total damage equals or exceeds the Pokémon's HP → it is Knocked Out!

Knocked Out Pokémon

A Pokémon is Knocked Out when its total accumulated damage equals or exceeds its HP.

What Happens When a Pokémon is Knocked Out

Discard the Pokémon

The Knocked Out Pokémon and all attached Energy Cards go to the losing player's discard pile.

Winner Claims a Prize Card

The winning player takes 1 Prize Card from their face-down prize stack and places it beside the Matrix (not back on it!).

Matrix Search Protocol

When a Trainer Card (e.g. Great Ball, Quick Ball) lets you search your deck, your "deck" is the Matrix itself. Use the Snake Pattern:

Start Top-Left

Begin at position [Row 1, Column 1] — top-left corner of your Matrix.

Scan in Snake Pattern

Flip and look at cards row by row in a snake (boustrophedon) pattern: left-to-right on odd rows, right-to-left on even rows.

Stop When Found

The moment you find the target card — stop! Do not reveal any more cards.

Re-Shuffle & Re-Lay

Shuffle all the other revealed cards well, then re-lay them face-down starting top-left in the snake pattern.

Place the Target Card

Put the found card face-up in any free spot of your choice on the Matrix, or play it immediately if applicable.

SNAKE SCAN PATTERN: Row 1 →→→→→→→→→ (left to right) Row 2 ←←←←←←←←← (right to left) Row 3 →→→→→→→→→ (left to right) ...continue until target card is found...

The game ends immediately when one of these conditions is met:

ConditionResult
A player collects their 6th Prize Card 🏆 That player WINS!
A player has no more Pokémon on the Matrix (all knocked out, none to replace) That player LOSES

11
Strategy Tips

🛡️ Defensive Anchoring

Keep backup attackers in Row 6 while they charge up. A Pokémon in Row 6 is protected from 50 damage — your opponent almost can't touch it.

⚡ Front = Maximum Damage

Move fully charged Pokémon to Row 1 for zero-reduction attacks. Row 1 is dangerous for YOUR Pokémon too, but the damage you deal is devastating.

🗺️ Path Clearing

Prioritize revealing cards that create diagonal corridors. Queen movement means diagonal paths are the fastest routes — and hardest for opponents to block.

⚡ Energy Route Planning

Move Pokémon so they pick up Energy cards on their way to the front line. Efficient routing means your attacker arrives at Row 1 already battle-ready.

🧬 Secure Evolutions

Head toward Evolution cards before your opponent blocks your path. A Stage 2 Pokémon changes the game — get there fast.

🎯 Many Basic Pokémon

Run plenty of Basic Pokémon in your deck. You need them for the startup reveal, and as replacement Active Pokémon when the current one is Knocked Out.

🧠 4-Space Planning

Remember: no Pokémon can cross the entire board in one move. Plan 2–3 turns ahead — advance step by step while charging Energy.

🏆 Prize Placement

Place won Prize Cards face-up in the center of your Matrix. They count as revealed cards and expand your movement network immediately.

Design Decisions

These design decisions document why the rules work the way they do.

// DE-101

Simplified Damage Formula

Problem: The original formula (deduction for BOTH Pokémon positions) created 0-damage situations frustrating for young players.

Decision: Only the defender's row determines protection.

Reason: Works like "armor" — easy to explain, and there's always a chance to deal some damage.

// DE-102

Maximum Movement of 4 Spaces

Problem: Unlimited queen movement allows a Pokémon to jump from Row 6 to Row 1 in a single turn, making positional strategy meaningless.

Decision: Maximum 4 spaces per turn.

Reason: Forces gradual advance; makes positioning matter and creates genuine tactical tension.

// DE-103

Player 1 Cannot Attack on Turn 1

Problem: Player 1 already has the advantage of going first.

Decision: Player 1 cannot attack on their very first turn.

Reason: Standard Pokémon TCG rule; balances the first-mover advantage.

// DE-104

Trainer Cards – Player Chooses

Problem: Auto-triggering a Trainer Card when passing over it creates edge cases (e.g. Supporter already played).

Decision: When a Pokémon passes over a Trainer Card, the player may choose: play it now OR leave it face-up on the Matrix.

Reason: Gives the player control; avoids ambiguous situations.

// DE-105

Prize Cards Go to Own Stack (Beside Matrix)

Problem: Putting won Prize Cards back on the Matrix is confusing.

Decision: Won Prize Cards go on a separate winner's stack beside the Matrix.

Reason: Cleaner tracking; no confusion if a Prize Card is itself a Pokémon.

// DE-106

Row Numbering Direction

Decision: Row 1 = Front Line = close to opponent = zero protection. Row 6 = Back Row = close to you = full protection.

Reason: Moving forward = more damage dealt; moving back = more protection received. Intuitive for all ages.
